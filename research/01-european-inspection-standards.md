

# European Bridge Inspection Standards — Research Report

## 1. Eurocodes / EN Standards for Bridge Assessment

### EN 1990–1999 Series (Structural Eurocodes)

The Eurocodes are a suite of 10 European standards (EN 1990 through EN 1999) for structural design, developed by CEN (European Committee for Standardization). They were primarily written for **new construction**, but their principles increasingly apply to the assessment of existing bridges:

- **EN 1990 (Eurocode 0):** Basis of Structural Design — defines reliability levels, limit states, load combinations. For existing bridges, the target reliability index (β) may be adjusted downward compared to new structures (e.g., β = 3.8 for new vs. β = 3.1 or lower for existing, depending on remaining life and consequence class).
- **EN 1991 (Eurocode 1):** Actions on Structures — Part 2 (EN 1991-2) specifically covers traffic loads on bridges, including Load Model 1 (LM1) for road bridges and load models for rail. Assessment of existing bridges often uses site-specific traffic data (WIM — Weigh-in-Motion) rather than the conservative notional models.
- **EN 1992 (Eurocode 2):** Concrete structures — used for assessing reinforced/prestressed concrete bridges.
- **EN 1993 (Eurocode 3):** Steel structures — Part 1-9 covers fatigue, critical for older steel bridges.
- **EN 1994 (Eurocode 4):** Composite steel-concrete structures.
- **EN 1995 (Eurocode 5):** Timber structures — relevant for historic timber bridges in Nordic countries.
- **EN 1996 (Eurocode 6):** Masonry — relevant for stone arch bridges (very common in Europe).
- **EN 1997 (Eurocode 7):** Geotechnical design — foundations, scour-related assessment.
- **EN 1998 (Eurocode 8):** Seismic design — relevant in southern Europe (Italy, Greece, Turkey).
- **EN 1999 (Eurocode 9):** Aluminium structures.

### Standards Specifically for Assessment of Existing Structures

There is **no single EN standard specifically for bridge inspection and assessment** at the European level. This is a recognized gap. However:

- **CEN/TC 250 Working Group "Assessment of Existing Structures"** has been working on a Technical Specification: **prEN 1990-2: Assessment and Retrofitting of Existing Structures**. This has been in development since approximately 2018-2019, building on the 2015 JRC (Joint Research Centre) Scientific and Policy Report "New European Technical Rules for the Assessment and Retrofitting of Existing Structures."
- **ISO 13822:2010** — "Bases for design of structures — Assessment of existing structures" is the international-level standard often referenced in Europe when EN standards are insufficient.
- **fib Bulletin 80 (2016):** "Partial factor methods for existing concrete structures" — widely referenced for concrete bridge assessment.
- **fib Model Code 2010** includes provisions for existing structures.

### Key Gap

The absence of a harmonized European standard for bridge assessment is precisely what motivated the **COST Action TU1406** (discussed below). Each country has developed its own system, leading to significant fragmentation.

## 2. Country-Specific Inspection Systems

### Germany: DIN 1076 and SIB-Bauwerke

**DIN 1076:1999-11 — "Engineering structures in connection with roads — Inspection and testing"**

This is one of Europe's most rigorous and well-documented bridge inspection standards.

**Inspection Types:**
- **Hauptprüfung (Principal Inspection):** Every 6 years, hands-on, close-range inspection of all bridge components. Requires access to all surfaces (undersides, bearings, etc.). The first Hauptprüfung occurs before or at handover.
- **Einfache Prüfung (Simple/Intermediate Inspection):** Every 6 years, offset by 3 years from the Hauptprüfung (i.e., inspections every 3 years alternating between principal and simple). Less intensive — primarily visual, may use binoculars rather than requiring scaffolding.
- **Laufende Beobachtung (Routine Surveillance):** Ongoing, typically every 6–12 months, performed by road maintenance staff. Superficial visual check from ground level / roadway.
- **Sonderprüfung (Special Inspection):** Triggered by events (flooding, collision, earthquake, excessive deterioration found in routine inspection).

**Condition Rating System (Zustandsnote):**

Germany uses a **1.0 to 4.0 scale** with increments of 0.5:

| Grade | Meaning | Description |
|-------|---------|-------------|
| 1.0 | Very good | No deficiencies |
| 1.5 | Good | Minor deficiencies, no impact on durability or safety |
| 2.0 | Satisfactory | Minor deficiencies, beginning impact on durability |
| 2.5 | Still satisfactory | Deficiencies affecting durability, monitoring needed |
| 3.0 | Insufficient | Safety or durability significantly impaired, action required within defined timeframe |
| 3.5 | Inadequate | Significant structural safety concerns, urgent action required |
| 4.0 | Unacceptable | Immediate safety risk, immediate restriction/closure |

The overall condition grade (Gesamtzustandsnote) is determined from individual component ratings using a defined worst-case aggregation method, not a simple average. The grade is composed from assessments of:
- **Standsicherheit (S)** — Structural stability/safety
- **Verkehrssicherheit (V)** — Traffic safety
- **Dauerhaftigkeit (D)** — Durability

Each defect is rated on all three criteria (S, V, D) on a scale of 0–4. The overall note is derived through a matrix system defined in the **RI-EBW-PRÜF** (Richtlinie zur einheitlichen Erfassung, Bewertung, Aufzeichnung und Auswertung von Ergebnissen der Bauwerksprüfungen nach DIN 1076).

**SIB-Bauwerke (Straßeninformationsbank Teilsystem Bauwerksdaten):**

This is the German federal **bridge management database system**. It stores:
- Bridge inventory data (location, type, dimensions, construction year, materials)
- Inspection results (all historical condition grades)
- Defect records with photos
- Maintenance and repair history
- Load capacity data

SIB-Bauwerke feeds into the prioritization of maintenance budgets at federal (Bund) and state (Land) levels. Germany manages approximately **39,500+ bridges** on federal highways (Bundesfernstraßen) through this system.

**ASB-ING (Anweisung Straßeninformationsbank — Ingenieurbauwerke):** The data standard/format for recording bridge data in SIB-Bauwerke.

### France: IQOA (Image Qualité des Ouvrages d'Art)

**IQOA** — developed by SETRA (now Cerema) for the French national road administration.

**Inspection Types:**
- **Visite IQOA (IQOA Visit):** Periodic assessment, typically every 3 years, systematic visual inspection. This is the core of the IQOA system.
- **Inspection Détaillée Périodique (IDP):** Detailed periodic inspection, every 6–9 years depending on bridge type and condition.
- **Inspection Détaillée Initiale (IDI):** Initial detailed inspection within the first years of service.
- **Surveillance Continue:** Ongoing surveillance during routine road maintenance.
- **Inspection Subaquatique:** Underwater inspection for bridges over waterways.

**IQOA Rating Scale:**

France uses a **combined letter-number system**:

| Class | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| 1 | Good apparent condition, only routine maintenance needed |
| 2 | Non-structural defects requiring specialized maintenance |
| 2E | Class 2 but urgent (E = "état") — defects could worsen rapidly |
| 3 | Structural defects requiring repair |
| 3U | Class 3 but urgent (U = "urgence") — structural defects requiring immediate attention, possible load restriction |

The "E" and "U" suffixes are critical for prioritization — they indicate urgency rather than just severity. This dual-axis approach (severity + urgency) is considered a strength of the French system.

**Assessment by Component Groups:**
IQOA rates bridges by structural zones/component families:
- Équipements (equipment: barriers, joints, drainage, waterproofing)
- Superstructure (tablier/deck)
- Appuis (supports: piers, abutments)
- Fondations (foundations)

France manages approximately **12,000+ bridges** on the national road network, plus tens of thousands more on departmental and municipal networks. The **LAGORA** database supports IQOA data management.

**Reference Documents:**
- "Surveillance et entretien courant des ouvrages d'art routiers" — guide by Cerema
- Fascicules IQOA: separate technical guides exist for each bridge type (prestressed concrete, masonry arch, steel, etc.)

### United Kingdom: BD 63, CS 450, CS 465

The UK system has undergone a naming transition. The old BD/BA (Design Manual for Roads and Bridges) references are being replaced by CS (Construction Standard) numbers under Highways England (now National Highways).

**Key Standards:**
- **BD 63/07** → now **CS 450 "Inspection of highway structures"** — defines inspection types and intervals
- **BA 63** → supplementary advice
- **BD 101** → now **CS 465 "Management of highway structures — A code of practice"** — the overarching bridge management code
- **CS 454 "Assessment of highway bridges and structures"** — for load capacity assessment

**Inspection Types (CS 450):**
- **Safety Inspection:** Frequent (typically every 2 weeks to 2 months), drive-by or walkthrough checking for obvious hazards.
- **General Inspection (GI):** Every 2 years, visual inspection from ground level without specialist access equipment.
- **Principal Inspection (PI):** Every 6 years, close-range hands-on inspection of all elements, requires access equipment (under-bridge inspection units, scaffolding, boats).
- **Special Inspection (SI):** Triggered by specific events or concerns.
- **Inspection for Assessment:** Detailed inspection to support a structural assessment (load capacity evaluation).

**Condition Rating:**

The UK uses a **severity + extent** system within the **Bridge Condition Index (BCI)**:

Individual element defects are scored:
- **Severity:** 1 (No significant defect), 2 (Minor), 3 (Moderate), 4 (Severe), 5 (Very severe / failure)
- **Extent:** A (less than 5%), B (5–20%), C (20–50%), D (more than 50%) of the element affected

These combine into two indices:
- **BCI_average:** Weighted average condition across all elements
- **BCI_critical:** Condition of the worst-performing critical element

Both BCI values range from **0 to 100**, where 100 = perfect condition. These are calculated using algorithms defined in the Bridge Management System.

**Bridge Management System:**
The UK uses **SMIS (Structures Management Information System)** — maintained by National Highways. Local authorities may use different systems. There are approximately **155,000 highway structures** in England alone (including bridges, culverts, retaining walls, sign gantries).

### Poland: GDDKiA System

**GDDKiA (Generalna Dyrekcja Dróg Krajowych i Autostrad)** — General Directorate for National Roads and Motorways.

**Standards:**
- Bridge inspection follows Polish implementations of Eurocodes (PN-EN series)
- **PN-EN 1991-2** for traffic loads
- The inspection methodology is defined in GDDKiA internal instructions, influenced by German DIN 1076

**Inspection Types:**
- **Przegląd bieżący (Current/Routine Inspection):** Annual, visual
- **Przegląd podstawowy (Basic Inspection):** Every 5 years
- **Przegląd rozszerzony (Extended Inspection):** Every 5 years (offset from basic), more detailed
- **Przegląd specjalny (Special Inspection):** As triggered

**Condition Rating:**

Poland uses a **0 to 5 scale**:

| Grade | Condition |
|-------|-----------|
| 0 | New / excellent |
| 1 | Good |
| 2 | Satisfactory |
| 3 | Unsatisfactory — repair needed |
| 4 | Bad — urgent repair needed |
| 5 | Pre-failure or failure — immediate action |

**Bridge Management System:**
Poland uses the **SGM (System Gospodarki Mostowej)** — Bridge Management System, developed in cooperation with the Road and Bridge Research Institute (IBDiM). It manages data for approximately **35,000+ bridge structures** on national roads.

### Nordic Countries

#### Denmark: DANBRO

**DANBRO** is the Danish Bridge Management System, operated by Vejdirektoratet (Danish Road Directorate).

- Inspections every **5 years** (principal) with routine inspections between.
- **Condition rating: 0–5 scale** (0 = no damage, 5 = critical)
- Each element is assessed, and defects are described using standardized damage types
- DANBRO includes a deterioration model and maintenance optimization module
- Manages approximately **2,400 state bridges** plus municipal bridges
- One of the earliest computerized BMS in Europe (developed since the 1980s)

#### Sweden: BaTMan (Bridge and Tunnel Management)

**BaTMan** — operated by Trafikverket (Swedish Transport Administration).

- URL: https://batman.trafikverket.se (publicly accessible for bridge data)
- **Condition Classification (CC): 0–3:**
  - CC 0: No deficiency
  - CC 1: Deficiency that does not require action within 10 years
  - CC 2: Deficiency requiring action within 3–10 years
  - CC 3: Deficiency requiring action within 3 years (or immediate for safety)
- **Inspection intervals:** Primary inspection every 6 years; annual surveillance.
- BaTMan manages approximately **31,000 bridges and 200+ tunnels** on state roads and railways.
- Notable for public data transparency.

#### Norway: Brutus

**Brutus** — operated by Statens vegvesen (Norwegian Public Roads Administration).

- Condition grades: **0–4 scale** (0 = no damage, 4 = critical)
- Inspections: routine (1–2 years), main inspection (every 5 years), special as needed
- Includes risk-based prioritization
- Manages approximately **18,000+ bridges and ferry quays**
- Strong focus on climate-related deterioration (freeze-thaw, marine exposure) given Norway's harsh conditions

#### Finland: Siltarekisteri (Bridge Register)

- Managed by Väylävirasto (Finnish Transport Infrastructure Agency)
- **Condition grades: 0–4** (similar to Nordic convention)
- Approximately **20,000+ bridges**

### Netherlands: RBK (Richtlijnen Beoordeling Kunstwerken)

**RBK** — "Guidelines for Assessment of Engineering Structures," published by Rijkswaterstaat (Dutch national infrastructure agency).

- **Inspection Types:**
  - Inspectie (Inspection): Regular visual inspection every 6 years
  - Nader Onderzoek (Detailed Investigation): When inspection reveals concerns
  - Toetsing (Assessment / Verification): Structural recalculation against current codes

- **Focus:** The Netherlands has particular challenges due to:
  - Large number of post-WWII bridges approaching end of design life
  - Heavy freight traffic (major logistics hub of Europe)
  - Water management — many movable bridges (approximately 900+)
  - Soil conditions (settlement, piled foundations)

- **NEN standards** (Dutch national standards harmonized with EN): NEN-EN 1990 through NEN-EN 1999

- **Condition Assessment:** Uses a consequence-class approach where structures are categorized by failure consequence, and the rigor of assessment scales accordingly.

- Rijkswaterstaat manages approximately **3,600+ bridges** on the main road network (Rijkswegennet).

## 3. Common Inspection Criteria Across European Systems

### Defect Categories

Despite national differences, most European systems categorize defects similarly:

**Structural Elements:**
- Deck/superstructure: cracking (flexural, shear, torsional), spalling, delamination, exposed reinforcement, corrosion, section loss, deflection/deformation, honeycombing
- Substructure (piers, abutments): settlement, tilting, cracking, scour undermining, collision damage
- Foundations: scour, settlement, erosion, deterioration of piles
- Bearings: malfunction, corrosion, displacement, elastomer deterioration
- Joints: leaking, damaged seals, debris accumulation, excessive movement
- Cables/tendons (prestressed/cable-stayed): corrosion, wire breaks, anchorage issues

**Non-Structural Elements:**
- Pavement/surfacing: rutting, potholes, cracking
- Waterproofing: failure, leakage signs
- Drainage: blocked, damaged, absent
- Barriers/parapets: collision damage, corrosion, non-compliance with current standards
- Coatings/protective systems: degradation, loss of effectiveness

### Scoring / Rating Approaches

Three dominant philosophies exist across Europe:

| Approach | Countries | Scale |
|----------|-----------|-------|
| Single overall grade | Germany, Poland | Numeric (1–4 or 0–5) |
| Severity + Urgency | France | Letter-number with urgency suffix |
| Element-level severity × extent | UK, Netherlands | Produces aggregate index (0–100) |
| Nordic simplified | Sweden, Norway, Denmark, Finland | Small numeric scale (0–3 or 0–4) |

### Standard Report Sections

A composite "typical European bridge inspection report" contains:

1. **Administrative Data:** Bridge ID, name, location (road, km marker, coordinates), owner/manager
2. **Structure Description:** Type (beam, arch, cable-stayed, etc.), materials, span lengths, width, skew, year built, designer/contractor
3. **Inspection Metadata:** Date, inspector name/qualifications, inspection type, weather, access methods used, equipment
4. **Component-by-Component Assessment:** Organized by structural system (foundations → substructure → superstructure → equipment)
5. **Defect Catalogue:** Each defect with: location, type, severity, extent, cause (if determinable), photos, sketch/drawing reference
6. **Condition Ratings:** Individual element ratings and overall structure rating
7. **Functional Assessment:** Load capacity status, clearance, width adequacy for current traffic
8. **Recommendations:** Required maintenance/repair actions, urgency/priority, estimated cost
9. **Appendices:** Photo log, drawings, test results (if any), previous inspection comparison

### Scour Assessment

Scour is handled with varying rigor:
- **UK (CS 469 / formerly BD 97):** Dedicated scour assessment standard with risk classification. Bridges over water require specific scour assessment during principal inspection.
- **Germany:** Scour addressed within DIN 1076 inspections; specific guidance in supplementary documents from BASt (Bundesanstalt für Straßenwesen).
- **France:** Underwater inspection (Inspection Subaquatique) is a defined inspection type under Cerema guidance.
- **Nordic countries:** High awareness due to spring flood conditions; Norway and Sweden have specific scour risk protocols.
- **General approach:** Most countries classify scour risk as low/medium/high and require monitoring or underwater inspection accordingly.

### Load Capacity Assessment

- Most countries distinguish between inspection (condition assessment) and assessment (structural recalculation).
- Load capacity assessment is triggered when: condition deteriorates below threshold, traffic loads increase, abnormal loads require routing, or on a periodic schedule.
- **Germany:** Nachrechnungsrichtlinie (Recalculation Guideline, 2011, updated 2015) — specific standard for recalculating existing bridges.
- **UK:** CS 454 (formerly BD 21/BD 86) — defines assessment loading and methods.
- **France:** Load capacity linked to classification of routes; heavier assessment for strategic routes.

### Redundancy

Structural redundancy is generally not scored as a standalone criterion in most European systems (unlike US NBIS). However, it is implicitly considered in:
- Risk assessment frameworks (non-redundant structures receive higher consequence ratings)
- Inspection frequency (fracture-critical / non-redundant members may require more frequent inspection)
- The UK system explicitly considers "structural form" in prioritization algorithms

## 4. COST Action TU1406 — Quality Specifications for Roadway Bridges

**Full Title:** "Quality specifications for roadway bridges, standardization at a European level"
**Duration:** 2015–2019
**Chair:** Prof. José C. Matos (University of Minho, Portugal)
**Participation:** 44 countries, 250+ researchers and practitioners
**Website:** https://www.tu1406.eu/

### Objectives

COST TU1406 was established specifically to address the fragmentation problem — each European country had its own bridge inspection and management system with no interoperability. The action aimed to:

1. Develop a **guideline for the establishment of Quality Control (QC) plans** for roadway bridges
2. Propose a standardized **performance indicator** framework
3. Develop a **performance goals** framework
4. Create a **decision-making** framework for bridge management

### Key Findings and Outputs

**Working Group 1 — Performance Indicators:**
- Catalogued performance indicators used across 30+ countries
- Identified that most countries use **condition-based indicators** (visual inspection results) as the primary metric
- Found that few countries systematically use **reliability-based indicators** (structural safety level expressed as reliability index β)
- Proposed a hierarchical performance indicator framework:
  - Level 0: Component-level condition
  - Level 1: System-level condition (structure overall)
  - Level 2: Network-level performance (for asset management)

**Working Group 2 — Performance Goals:**
- Defined performance goals in terms of: Safety, Availability, Economy, Environment
- Proposed relating performance indicators to target reliability levels per EN 1990

**Working Group 3 — Establishment of QC Plans:**
- Developed a **Quality Control framework** with three tiers:
  - Inspection (data collection)
  - Assessment (analysis of data)
  - Decision (maintenance/repair prioritization)
- Proposed **Key Performance Indicators (KPIs)** and **Key Performance Thresholds (KPTs)**

**Working Group 4 — Implementation:**
- Case studies across participating countries
- Benchmarking of national systems against the proposed framework

### Major Publications

- **"Performance Indicators for Roadway Bridges of Cost Action TU1406"** — comprehensive report cataloguing indicators across Europe
- **"Standardization of quality control plans for roadway bridges"** — the main guideline document
- Multiple journal papers in *Structure and Infrastructure Engineering*, *Structural Engineering International*, and conference proceedings (IABMAS, IALCCE)
- Final conference proceedings (2019)

### Key Recommendation

The overarching recommendation was to move toward a **performance-based** bridge management approach across Europe, where:
- Performance indicators are defined consistently
- Threshold values trigger defined actions
- Decision-making follows a transparent, comparable methodology
- Results are translatable across national borders

This work directly feeds into CEN standardization efforts and the development of the next generation of Eurocodes.

## 5. BRIME (Bridge Management in Europe) Project

**Full Title:** Bridge Management in Europe
**Funding:** EU 4th Framework Programme (Transport RTD Programme)
**Duration:** 1998–2001 (Project Reference: RO-97-SC.2220)
**Partners:** TRL (UK, coordinator), LCPC (France), BASt (Germany), ZAG (Slovenia), IBDIM (Poland), CEDEX (Spain)

### Context

BRIME was one of the earliest EU-funded efforts to compare and potentially harmonize bridge management practices across Europe. It predated COST TU1406 by nearly two decades.

### Key Deliverables and Findings

**Deliverable D1 — Review of current procedures for assessing load carrying capacity:**
- Documented how each partner country assessed load capacity
- Found major discrepancies in safety factors, load models, and material property assumptions
- A bridge deemed acceptable in one country could fail assessment in another

**Deliverable D2 — Review of bridge condition surveys:**
- Compared inspection procedures across partner countries
- Documented the different rating scales (see Section 2 above)
- Found that inspector subjectivity was a major issue — different inspectors rating the same bridge could give significantly different scores

**Deliverable D3 — Bridge Management Systems:**
- Compared DANBRO (Denmark), PONTIS/BRIDGIT (US, for context), and national systems
- Found that most European BMS lacked sophisticated deterioration models
- Recommended development of network-level optimization tools

**Deliverable D6 — Experimental assessment methods:**
- Evaluated non-destructive testing (NDT) methods: radar, impact-echo, ultrasonic, etc.
- Assessed role of proof load testing for capacity verification
- Recommended greater use of monitoring and NDT to supplement visual inspection

**Deliverable D14 — Final Report:**
- Proposed a framework for a common European approach to bridge management
- Recommended reliability-based assessment as the common methodology
- Identified training and certification of inspectors as a critical need

### Legacy

BRIME's work informed:
- The development of national standards in several countries
- The scope and objectives of COST TU1406 (which picked up where BRIME left off)
- CEN technical committee work on assessment of existing structures
- Multiple subsequent EU projects: SAMARIS (2003–2006), SUSTAINABLE BRIDGES (2003–2007), MAINLINE (2011–2014)

## 6. Typical European Bridge Inspection Report Structure

### Standard Report Sections

Based on the synthesis of multiple national systems, a "consensus" European bridge inspection report contains:

**1. Cover Page / Header**
- Bridge identification number (national code)
- Bridge name / road designation
- Location (coordinates, route, km marker, municipality)
- Inspection date and type
- Inspector name, organization, qualification level

**2. Bridge Description / Inventory Data**
- Structural type (e.g., 3-span continuous prestressed concrete box girder)
- Year of construction, designer, contractor
- Key dimensions: total length, span arrangement, width, height clearances
- Materials
- Foundation type
- Waterway data (if over water): normal/flood levels, catchment
- Traffic data: AADT, % heavy vehicles, speed limit

**3. Previous Inspection Summary**
- Date and type of last inspection
- Previous condition rating
- Actions recommended previously and their completion status
- Trend analysis (improving / stable / deteriorating)

**4. Current Inspection Details**
- Access methods used (MEWP, under-bridge unit, scaffolding, rope access, boat)
- Weather conditions
- Areas not inspected and reasons
- Equipment / instruments used

**5. Findings by Element Group**

Typically organized in this order:
- 5.1 Foundations (including scour assessment)
- 5.2 Substructure (abutments, piers, retaining walls)
- 5.3 Bearings
- 5.4 Superstructure / deck (beams, slabs, arches, cables)
- 5.5 Expansion joints
- 5.6 Waterproofing / surfacing
- 5.7 Drainage system
- 5.8 Parapets / barriers / safety features
- 5.9 Approaches and embankments
- 5.10 Other features (lighting, signage, utilities)

Each element entry includes:
- Condition description (narrative)
- Defect type (from standardized catalogue)
- Severity rating
- Extent (area/percentage affected)
- Cause assessment
- Photo references (numbered, cross-referenced)
- Sketch/drawing with defect locations marked

**6. Condition Rating Summary**
- Individual element/component ratings
- Overall structure condition rating
- Comparison with previous rating

**7. Load Capacity Status**
- Current load capacity assessment status
- Any restrictions in place
- Need for reassessment

**8. Recommendations and Priority**
- Required maintenance actions
- Required repair actions
- Priority / urgency classification
- Estimated timeframe for action
- Budget-level cost estimate (in some systems)

**9. Appendices**
- Photo log (all photos, numbered, with descriptions)
- Defect drawings / sketches
- Test results (concrete cover, half-cell potential, carbonation, chloride, etc.)
- Monitoring data (if instrumented)
- Historical comparison data

### Scoring Scales Summary

| Country | Scale | Type | Notes |
|---------|-------|------|-------|
| Germany | 1.0–4.0 | Numeric (0.5 steps) | 1.0 best, 4.0 worst. Composed from S/V/D sub-scores |
| France | 1, 2, 2E, 3, 3U | Alpha-numeric | Urgency suffixes (E, U) are key differentiator |
| UK | 1–5 severity × A–D extent → BCI 0–100 | Element-level matrix → aggregate index | Two BCIs: average and critical |
| Poland | 0–5 | Numeric | 0 best, 5 worst |
| Denmark | 0–5 | Numeric | 0 best, 5 worst |
| Sweden | 0–3 (CC) | Condition class | 0 best, 3 worst |
| Norway | 0–4 | Numeric | 0 best, 4 worst |
| Finland | 0–4 | Numeric | 0 best, 4 worst |
| Netherlands | Consequence class-based | Risk framework | Not a simple linear scale |
| Italy | 1–5 (post-Morandi) | Multi-level | New system since 2020 "Linee Guida" |
| Switzerland (SIA 269) | 1–4 | Condition state | Referenced for completeness |

### Urgency / Priority Communication

European systems generally use one of these approaches:

1. **Embedded in the condition grade** (Germany: grade 3.5+ implies urgency; Poland: grade 4-5 implies urgency)
2. **Separate urgency suffix** (France: E and U suffixes)
3. **Separate urgency/priority field** (UK: recommendations include priority category, often 1–4 or Immediate/Urgent/Routine/Programmed)
4. **Risk-based priority** (Netherlands: combines probability of failure × consequence)

### Mandatory vs. Optional Data

**Universally mandatory:**
- Bridge identification and location
- Inspection date, type, inspector
- Element-by-element condition assessment
- Overall condition rating
- Photographs of significant defects
- Recommendations for action

**Commonly mandatory but varying in detail:**
- Load capacity status
- Previous inspection comparison
- Scour assessment (for bridges over water)
- Clearance measurements

**Optional / country-dependent:**
- Detailed cost estimates for repairs
- Deterioration modeling / predicted future condition
- Environmental impact assessment
- Risk scoring
- Remaining service life estimate
- Monitoring recommendations

## Summary of Key Gaps and Trends

1. **No harmonized European standard exists** for bridge inspection and assessment — this remains the single biggest gap.
2. **COST TU1406 made significant progress** toward harmonization but its recommendations are not yet codified in EN standards.
3. **Inspector subjectivity** remains a recognized problem across all countries — efforts toward automation (drone-based inspection, AI-assisted defect detection) are accelerating.
4. **Performance-based approaches** are the direction of travel, moving from purely condition-based to reliability-based assessment.
5. **Digital twins and BIM for existing bridges** are emerging as the next frontier in several countries (Netherlands, Germany, UK leading).
6. **Post-Morandi (Genoa 2018)** has accelerated reform, particularly in Italy (new mandatory classification system) and across Europe (increased political attention to aging infrastructure).
